<p style="margin:0in 0in .0001pt;"><b><u><font size="2" face="Times New Roman" style="font-size:10.0pt;font-weight:bold;">Comments from the Chair and the Prez</font></u></b></p>

<p style="margin:0in 0in .0001pt;"><font size="2" face="Times New Roman">&nbsp;</font></p>

<p style="margin:0in 0in .0001pt;"><font size="2" face="Times New Roman" style="font-size:10.0pt;">Since this is the last
newsletter of 2004, John and Gary felt it important to recognize the positive
things that have happened to this industry this year, namely:</font></p>

<p style="margin:0in 0in .0001pt;"><font size="2" face="Times New Roman">&nbsp;</font></p>

<p style="margin:0in 0in .0001pt;"><b><font size="2" face="Times New Roman" style="font-size:10.0pt;font-weight:bold;">The U.S. Ethanol Hits another
Monthly&#160; Production Record</font></b></p>

<p style="margin:0in 0in .0001pt;"><font size="2" face="Times New Roman" style="font-size:10.0pt;">The US ethanol industry is setting
new production levels month after month, driving forward to produce nearly 3.5
billion gallons this year.&#160; Currently 81
ethanol plants are operating across the country and approximately 19 more are
under construction. The American Petroleum Institute estimates 30% of America&#146;s
fuel will contain ethanol in 2004.</font></p>

<p style="margin:0in 0in .0001pt;"><font size="2" face="Times New Roman">&nbsp;</font></p>

<p style="margin:0in 0in .0001pt;"><b><font size="2" face="Times New Roman" style="font-size:10.0pt;font-weight:bold;">Costly Oil Propels Ethanol and
Biodiesel in 2004</font></b></p>

<p style="margin:0in 0in .0001pt;"><font size="2" face="Times New Roman" style="font-size:10.0pt;">With crude oil hitting historic
highs, the demand for ethanol has reached unprecedented levels.&#160; Discretionary use of ethanol has dramatically
increased to extend gasoline supplies that are sometimes in short supply.&#160; This additional use of ethanol is helping to
keep ethanol demand high in spite of new production coming on line.</font></p>

<p style="margin:0in 0in .0001pt;"><font size="2" face="Times New Roman">&nbsp;</font></p>

<p style="margin:0in 0in .0001pt;"><b><font size="2" face="Times New Roman" style="font-size:10.0pt;font-weight:bold;">President Bush Signs Critical
Ethanol Tax Measures Into Law</font></b></p>

<p style="margin:0in 0in .0001pt;"><font size="2" face="Times New Roman" style="font-size:10.0pt;">The 2004 JOBS bill contains
important tax provisions designed to enhance and extend the ethanol tax
incentive through 2010.&#160; The VEETEC
portion of this bill resolves the Highway Trust Fund issue by eliminating any
impact of the ethanol program on the Highway Trust Fund.&#160; It provides new flexibility as to how the
incentive is claimed and will make new markets for ethanol, including E-85 and
E-diesel.&#160; Indeed, the JOBS bill is in
many respects the most pro-ethanol legislation since the Clean Air Act
Amendments of 1990.</font></p>

<p style="margin:0in 0in .0001pt;"><font size="2" face="Times New Roman">&nbsp;</font></p>

<p style="margin:0in 0in .0001pt;"><b><font size="2" face="Times New Roman" style="font-size:10.0pt;font-weight:bold;">Wisconsin Legislature to
Consider Mandatory Ethanol Blending in 2005</font></b></p>

<p style="margin:0in 0in .0001pt;"><font size="2" face="Times New Roman" style="font-size:10.0pt;">John Malchine, along with a broad
coalition of other ethanol supporters has prepared legislation which is
expected to be introduced during the 2005 legislative session.&#160; If enacted, mandatory blending of ethanol in
Wisconsin would be expanded to the entire state.&#160; Currently only non-attainment areas in and
around Milwaukee are required to use ethanol.&#160;
The legislation is being promoted as <u>good</u> for the environment, <u>good</u>
for energy independence and <u>good</u> for Wisconsin corn producers.</font></p>

<p style="margin:0in 0in .0001pt;"><font size="2" face="Times New Roman">&nbsp;</font></p>

<p style="margin:0in 0in .0001pt;"><b><font size="2" face="Times New Roman" style="font-size:10.0pt;font-weight:bold;">New Breakthrough in Ethanol Process
Technology</font></b></p>

<p style="margin:0in 0in .0001pt;"><font size="2" face="Times New Roman" style="font-size:10.0pt;">Companies that produce enzymes
for the ethanol industry are now reporting a significant breakthrough in the
development of a new generation-type enzyme that attacks and digests starch
molecules without the need for cooking and liquefaction at the beginning of the
process.&#160; This is expected to result in a
significant reduction in the total amount of energy needed to process ethanol.&#160; Other benefits from this technology include
reduced infection, higher starch conversions to ethanol, higher amounts of
protein in distillers.&#160; The front end of
the ethanol production process will be forever changed as we now know it.</font></p>

<p style="margin:0in 0in .0001pt;"><font size="2" face="Times New Roman">&nbsp;</font></p>

<p style="margin:0in 0in .0001pt;"><b><font size="2" face="Times New Roman" style="font-size:10.0pt;font-weight:bold;">Seed Corn Industry Partners
with Ethanol Industry to Improve Production Process</font></b></p>

<p style="margin:0in 0in .0001pt;"><font size="2" face="Times New Roman" style="font-size:10.0pt;">Monsanto and Pioneer Seeds have
both established programs to emphasize the planting of highly fermentable
starch varieties of corn.&#160; Both companies
have identified within their own production lines the varieties that have shown
up to 10% more ethanol per bushel of corn.&#160;
Over time, ethanol yields from specific varieties will continue to
increase as genetic selection continues.</font></p>
